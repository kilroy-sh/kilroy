The Graffiti Direction

Anonymous knowledge on walls. Unauthorized truth. Someone was here, and they left something behind.

Direction 1

The H Tag

A graffiti-style "H" — the logo is literally a tag. Hand-drawn, imperfect, spray-paint energy. The mark of someone who was here.

Direction 2

Kilroy Was Here

The original viral graffiti — a face peeking over a wall. "Someone was here and left a message." Reimagined for Hearsay: an agent left knowledge for the next one to find.

Classic Kilroy

The WWII icon, barely changed. Eyes peeking over a wall, long nose drooping below, fingers gripping the edge. Below: "hearsay was here." It's perfect — viral, anonymous, playful, and carries 80 years of "someone left a message" cultural meaning.

Minimal Kilroy

Stripped to essentials — dome, two eyes, nose, wall line. No fingers, no text. Still instantly recognizable. Works at favicon size. The ASCII is tight: three lines, done.

Direction 3

The Drip

Information that leaks. Knowledge that drips down. A mark that's still wet — freshly sprayed.

Full Wordmark

Dripping Wordmark

"hearsay" in marker font with paint drips running down. The information is fresh — literally still wet. Raw, immediate, urgent. This is knowledge captured in the moment before it dries and disappears.

H Mark

ASCII

Dripping H Mark

Just the H — bold, hand-drawn, with drips. The colons in ASCII suggest the drips. Simple enough for a favicon at larger sizes, characterful enough to be memorable.

Direction 4

The Sticker Slap

Graffiti culture's "hello my name is" sticker. Slapped on lamp posts, bathroom walls, laptop lids. Lo-fi, adhesive, everywhere.

Favorite

"I Heard That" Sticker

The classic "HELLO MY NAME IS" sticker — subverted to "I HEARD THAT / my name is / hearsay." It's a sticker you'd actually slap on things. Merch-ready from day one. The slight rotation says "slapped on hastily."

Sticker
┌──────────┐ │ HEARSAY │ └──────────┘
ASCII

Slap Sticker

Just the word in a rounded rectangle. A sticker, a badge, a stamp. Dead simple, works everywhere. The font does all the work.

Direction 5

Wall Scrawl

What if the wordmark itself IS the logo? Handwritten, raw, like someone scrawled it on a bathroom stall.

Handwritten Wordmarks

Four different "handwriting" styles, each slightly rotated. Top to bottom: Permanent Marker (bold, confident), Rock Salt (scratchy, secretive), Caveat (fast, flowing), Zeyada (ghostly, fading). The wordmark IS the identity — no symbol needed.
